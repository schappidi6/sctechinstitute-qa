Starting a Career in AI in 2026: Roles, Salaries, and Realistic Paths

What "AI work" actually maps to in US labor data, what those roles pay according to the most recent BLS release, and what the credible paths in look like in 2026.

"How do I become an AI developer?" is the most common career question we hear from prospective students. The honest answer starts with a fact most career-change articles skip: the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics doesn't publish a separate occupation called "AI Developer" or "AI Engineer." AI work in 2026 is distributed across at least three BLS occupational classifications, each with different median wages, growth projections, and education norms.

This guide uses the most recent BLS data (May 2024 wages, August 2025 employment projections) to give you a realistic picture of what AI roles pay, how fast they're growing, and what kinds of preparation employers are actually expecting in 2025–2026.

Where AI Work Lives in the BLS Classification System

BLS organizes occupations under SOC (Standard Occupational Classification) codes. The roles that overlap with what people call "AI work" sit primarily under three SOC codes [1]:

  • Software Developers (SOC 15-1252) — the largest bucket, including most engineers building applications that integrate AI/ML models.
  • Data Scientists (SOC 15-2051) — analytics roles that increasingly use machine learning.
  • Computer and Information Research Scientists (SOC 15-1221) — the closest fit for senior AI/ML researchers and people doing original research.

This matters because anyone telling you "the median AI developer salary is $X" is either citing a specific job board's user-reported data (Levels.fyi, Glassdoor) or making it up. The closest authoritative US numbers come from these three SOC codes.

What These Roles Actually Pay (May 2024 BLS data)

Here are the verified median annual wages from the May 2024 Occupational Employment and Wage Statistics, the most recent BLS release as of this writing:

Occupation (SOC code) Median wage Lowest 10% Highest 10%
Software Developers (15-1252) [2] $133,080 under $79,850 over $211,450
Data Scientists (15-2051) [3] $112,590 under $63,650 over $194,410
Computer & Information Research Scientists (15-1221) [4] $140,910 under $80,670 over $232,120

Notice the spread. The 10th-percentile software developer earns under $80,000 a year — that's a real entry-level number, often outside major tech hubs. The 90th-percentile computer/information research scientist earns over $232,000 — that's a senior researcher at a top company. Both are part of "AI careers." Where you land depends on role, location, employer, and experience.

Reality check on "AI engineer" salary numbers you see online: the popular figures circulating in 2025–2026 (e.g., "$145K average AI engineer salary") come from job-board self-reported data — not from BLS. They reflect a specific subset of self-identified AI engineers (often in major hubs, often at AI-focused companies). They aren't a national wage estimate. Treat them as one data point, not the data point.

How Fast Is the Field Growing?

BLS released its 2024–2034 Employment Projections in August 2025. The projections for the AI-adjacent occupations are well above the 3% all-occupations average [5]:

  • Software Developers, QA Analysts, and Testers: employment projected to grow 15% from 2024 to 2034 — about five times the all-occupations rate. About 129,200 openings projected per year on average. [2]
  • Data Scientists: projected to grow about 34% over the same decade — one of the four fastest-growing occupations BLS tracks. About 23,400 openings projected per year. [3]

Two things to note about projections in general: BLS projections assume "normal" macroeconomic conditions and historical trend continuation. Recessions, AI-driven productivity shifts, and offshoring patterns can all alter the actual trajectory.

What Employers Are Asking For (Indeed and LinkedIn 2025)

Two non-government data sources help round out the picture:

Indeed Hiring Lab reports the share of US job postings mentioning AI hit 4.2% in December 2025 — the highest since the indicator began in 2019, and 134% above the February 2020 baseline [6]. By sector at end of 2025, AI mentions were heaviest in Data & Analytics (~45% of postings), Marketing (~15%), and HR (~9%) [6].

LinkedIn's "Jobs on the Rise" 2025 report ranked "AI Engineer" the #1 fastest-growing job in the United States [7]. That ranking is based on LinkedIn-platform job-title growth rates, not on a labor-economics measure — useful as a directional signal of where hiring momentum is.

The Stack Overflow Developer Survey 2024 found 76% of developers are currently using or planning to use AI tools in their development process (up from 70% in 2023); 62% are currently using them [8]. Top uses: writing code (82% of AI users), search (67.5%), debugging (56.7%) [8]. AI-tooling fluency is becoming table stakes for general software roles, not just AI-specialist roles.

What Education Path Actually Works?

BLS describes the typical entry path for data scientists as: "data scientists typically need at least a bachelor's degree in mathematics, statistics, computer science, or a related field" — and notes some employers prefer master's or doctoral degrees [3]. The "typically" matters: it describes the modal hire, not a hard requirement.

For software developers, the BLS occupational outlook similarly cites a bachelor's as typical entry-level [2]. Outside of BLS-published data, hiring practices in 2025–2026 have visibly shifted at parts of the tech industry — many companies now consider candidates with non-traditional credentials (bootcamps, diplomas, self-taught portfolios) for software developer roles. Whether they consider those credentials for AI-specific senior roles is more variable.

The honest summary:

  • Bachelor's degree remains the BLS-described "typical" entry credential for both software developer and data scientist roles. It clears HR filters at degree-required companies.
  • Diplomas, bootcamps, and self-study can produce hireable junior software developers, especially at startups and SaaS companies that screen on portfolio/work-sample over credential. Their effectiveness for AI-specialist roles depends heavily on what you build and demonstrate.
  • Advanced degrees (master's, PhD) are typical for research-scientist roles at the top of the wage distribution.

What This Means for People Considering a Switch

The picture supported by current verified data:

  1. The roles labeled "AI" pay in the high five to low six figures at median, with substantial upside at senior levels — verified by BLS May 2024 OEWS data.
  2. Demand is growing meaningfully faster than the rest of the economy — verified by BLS 2024–2034 projections.
  3. The credentialing landscape is real but less rigid for software developer roles than for data scientist or research scientist roles.
  4. The single biggest predictor of whether a non-traditional candidate gets hired is what they can show — projects, code, deployed work — not just where they trained.

SC Technological Institute's 12-week Artificial Intelligence Developer Diploma is one credential path, designed for working professionals with prior tech background. It's not a substitute for a bachelor's degree at degree-required employers — but for people deepening existing technical skills toward AI-applied software developer roles, it's the kind of focused program that produces a portfolio + credential combination.

Whichever path you choose, the people landing AI roles in 2026 share three things: real foundations in software engineering, demonstrated work they can show, and the willingness to honestly explain what they don't yet know in interviews. Those are independent of which credential you hold.
