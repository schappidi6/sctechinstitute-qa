How to Evaluate a Tech Education Program: Lessons from the 2024 CFPB BloomTech Action

In April 2024 a federal regulator banned a major coding bootcamp from consumer lending and ordered restitution. Here's what that case teaches anyone considering a tech bootcamp or diploma program in 2026.

The bootcamp industry's reputation has earned its scrutiny. In April 2024, the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) took action against BloomTech (formerly Lambda School) and CEO Austen Allred for "deceiving students about the cost of loans and making false claims about graduates' hiring rates" [1]. The case is the clearest published illustration of what to look for — and what to walk away from — when evaluating any tech-education program.

This guide uses that case as the anchor. Every claim is sourced; the questions you should ask any program (including SC Technological Institute) are at the end.

What Actually Happened in the BloomTech Case

According to the CFPB's April 17, 2024 enforcement action [1]:

  • BloomTech "advertised job-placement rates of 71 to 86 percent on its website" — but its CEO knew internal metrics showed actual placement rates closer to 50 percent, with some cohorts as low as 30 percent.
  • BloomTech originated "more than 11,000 income share loans since 2017," with average finance charges of approximately $4,000, while marketing them to students as "not loans."
  • The CFPB permanently banned BloomTech from consumer lending activities and banned Allred personally from any student lending activities for 10 years.
  • The order included approximately $164,000 in civil money penalties and required restitution to harmed students.

This is not a critique of bootcamps as a category — it's a documented case of one of the larger players in the space being found to have systematically misrepresented outcomes and loan terms. The patterns the CFPB flagged are repeatable elsewhere; that's why they're useful as a checklist.

The Regulatory Landscape You Should Know About

Three layers of oversight apply to tech-education programs in the United States. Knowing which apply to a program you're considering tells you what consumer protections exist if something goes wrong.

1. Federal: CFPB and FTC

The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau has jurisdiction over financial products marketed to students (including ISAs and tuition loans). The 2024 BloomTech action is its highest-profile use of that authority in the bootcamp space [1].

The Federal Trade Commission has authority over deceptive education marketing. In October 2021, the FTC issued a Notice of Penalty Offenses Concerning Education to 70 for-profit colleges, putting them on notice that misleading job-placement and earnings claims expose them to civil penalties of up to $43,792 per violation [2]. The FTC reported more than $63 million in education-sector consumer refunds in 2024 [3].

2. National Accreditation: ACCSC and equivalent

The Accrediting Commission of Career Schools and Colleges (ACCSC) is recognized by the U.S. Department of Education and the Council for Higher Education Accreditation. ACCSC accredits career-focused post-secondary institutions; its accredited-school directory is publicly searchable [4]. National accreditation generally enables Title IV federal financial aid (FAFSA).

3. State Licensure

States separately regulate non-public post-secondary institutions. In Florida, that's the Commission for Independent Education (CIE), a unit of the Florida Department of Education. CIE-licensed schools must file annual reports including enrollment, graduation, and placement data each November and undergo annual on-site visits [5]. CIE is recognized by NC-SARA, the State Authorization Reciprocity Agreement [6].

State licensure is a meaningful baseline — it means a state regulator has reviewed curriculum, faculty qualifications, financial responsibility, and consumer protections — but it is not equivalent to national accreditation. Most state-licensed-only institutions are not Title IV-eligible. For SCTI specifically, this distinction is documented openly in our FAQ: licensed by Florida CIE (License #13616), not regionally or nationally accredited [7].

The Questions to Ask Any Program Before Enrolling

From the BloomTech case and the regulatory landscape above, here is the actual checklist:

1. "What's your published placement rate, and how is it defined?"

Marketing copy that says "100% placement" or "job guarantee" without published methodology is the single biggest warning sign — that is precisely the pattern the CFPB cited in the BloomTech action [1]. The right answer specifies:

  • Time window (3 months? 6 months? 12 months?)
  • Salary floor (any job? any tech job? a job above $X?)
  • Whether the rate counts students who didn't complete the program
  • Who audits the number

2. "Are you CIRR-verified, or how is your placement rate audited?"

The Council on Integrity in Results Reporting (CIRR) is a third-party-verified bootcamp outcomes-reporting standard. CIRR member schools publish standardized 6-month and 12-month employment-rate reports per cohort, with verified salary distributions [8]. If a bootcamp is not CIRR-verified, ask what audit process its self-reported numbers go through. "Our internal data" is not an audit.

3. "What financial product is this?"

If a program offers an Income Share Agreement (ISA) or any deferred-tuition product, treat it as a loan and ask:

  • What are the total finance charges across the life of the agreement?
  • What's the maximum I'll repay?
  • What happens if I'm laid off, change careers, or take parental leave?
  • Is the ISA disclosed under Truth in Lending Act standards?

The CFPB found BloomTech misrepresented ISAs as "not loans" — a pattern they're now actively enforcing against [1]. If a program's financial product reads like a loan, it is one. Read the documents.

4. "What's your accreditation or state licensure status?"

Three valid answers, in order of regulatory weight:

  • Regionally accredited (the strongest credential, typical of universities and some community colleges).
  • Nationally accredited (e.g., ACCSC) — Title IV-eligible at most accredited institutions.
  • State-licensed only (e.g., Florida CIE) — meaningful baseline, but not Title IV-eligible at most institutions.

An "unaccredited and unlicensed" program is a fourth answer that should make you ask hard questions about consumer recourse.

5. "What's your refund policy?"

Look for a tiered refund policy that gives meaningful refunds during the first portion of the program. Florida CIE-licensed institutions (including SCTI) follow a 4-tier refund structure required by state regulation [7]. Programs without clearly published refund schedules are programs without consumer protection.

6. "Can I see the contract before I commit?"

Read the enrollment agreement before signing. Look specifically for:

  • Refund schedule (tied to # of weeks or hours completed)
  • Mandatory arbitration clauses (these limit your right to sue)
  • Career-services obligations (what they will and won't do)
  • What happens if the program shuts down mid-cohort

7. "Who teaches, and what's their actual experience?"

Check whether instructors have shipped real production work in the field, not just credentials. Ask to see two or three instructor profiles before enrolling.

Walk away if:

  • The program won't disclose how its placement rate is calculated.
  • An ISA is offered without clear total-cost disclosure under TILA.
  • "Pay nothing until you get a job" is the marketing pitch — those programs typically have higher total cost than upfront-tuition models.
  • The program is neither accredited nor state-licensed.
  • Reviews online are uniformly glowing or uniformly hostile (real programs have a mix).
  • Sales pressure includes "enroll today for $X off."

How to Verify a Florida-Based Program's CIE License

For any Florida-licensed program (including SCTI), you can independently verify the license through the FL Department of Education's CIE search tool:

  • Go to web02.fldoe.org/cie/searchschools/SchoolSearch.aspx
  • Search by school name or license number
  • Confirm the license is current and in good standing

SCTI's CIE License is #13616 [7]. We encourage prospective students to verify it independently.
